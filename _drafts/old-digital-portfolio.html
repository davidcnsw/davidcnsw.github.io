This page offers a digital portfolio of my work. I included several categories of writing: articles, web projects, technical writing, marketing mailings, and press releases. I also added some explanation about why I selected these pieces.

Articles

"Podcasting: A New Layer of Communication"

I wrote this article for the January 2007 issue of STC 's Intercom magazine. The article explores how podcasting can be used as another tool in your mix of marketing deliverables. To research the topic, I created my own podcast and learned quite a bit in the process.

Here's an excerpt from the article: "Podcasting refers to audio files on the Internet that you can download to your MP3 player and listen to on the go. You can add files to your MP3 player the old-fashioned way—by downloading the files and dragging them into your player—or you can use podcatching applications such as Juice, iTunes, or FeedStation to automatically download your subscribed podcasts to your computer.

"The idea is that you wake up in the morning and find that your podcatcher has downloaded the latest content, which you can easily transfer to your MP3 player. Then as you make that long commute to work, run on the treadmill at the gym, or take a relaxing walk in the park, you pop in your earbuds (or, in your car, switch on the FM transmitter) and listen to your podcasts." (Read the PDF.)

One reader liked this article so much she wrote me saying, "Tom, I just finished reading your article entitled Podcasting: A New Layer of Communication in Intercom. I thoroughly enjoyed reading it and learning more about Podcasting. It was one of the most informative, best written, and practical articles I've read. After reading it, I am ready to go get the equipment I need to Podcast, download some Podcasts, and start enjoying this medium.

"Your article explained how to do it all! And if my company decides they need something like this, I'm ready to create it for them. I think it would be great if all the STC chapters loaded their chapter meetings into Podcasts that we could listen to."

This was my first article in Intercom, and it was somewhat of an experiment. I did at least half of the research by listening to podcasts on blogging, and then I wove together the quotes with my own analysis and commentary. It worked well and was selected as the cover story in Intercom for the Sept./Oct 2006 issue.

Here's an excerpt: "Though it may be a small percentage of all blogging, corporate blogging is rapidly gaining popularity as a way for companies to connect with their customers and increase internal communication.

"About 5 percent of Fortune 500 companies are blogging, particularly IT companies, according to the Fortune 500 Business Blogging Wiki. The biggest bloggers include Sun Microsystems, with 2,000 employees blogging, and Microsoft, with approximately 3,000 bloggers." (Read the PDF.)

"Should You Sit Near SMEs?"

This is a small article I wrote for TechCraft -- an online newsletter for the Technical Writers of India mailing list. Although the article is brief, the concept is crucial: proximity is a major factor in communication with SMEs and users. Here's an excerpt: "I recently had the experience of sitting in contrasting locations on IT projects: first in the same cube as the lead subject matter expert (SME), and then in my own cube in another room, separated from other SMEs. As a result, I've thought a lot about where technical writers should sit during an IT project."

To read the original source (Yahoo login required), enter TechCraft and scroll to the Sep 2007 issue. Or read the Tech Craft PDF directly here. Scroll to page 13 (the "Legally Bland" section).

"Adding a Blog to Your Chapter Website"

When I converted the Suncoast chapter website into a blog, it caught the attention of several STC leaders and they asked me to write up an article for the April 2006 edition of Tieline (a publication for STC leaders).

Here's an excerpt from the article: "The many advantages of blogs make the medium too attractive to pass up. The content is XML-based and each post is formatted in an RSS feed. RSS feeds allow your content to be automatically syndicated across hundreds of sites and newsreaders.

"Readers can subscribe to your site's feed and receive the content in their newsreaders (some examples of newsreaders are Bloglines, Google Reader, NewsGator, and FeedDemon). "Social Rules for Creating a Style Guide"

After participating in an effort to update our department's style guide, I learned a lot about the social aspects of change in a department. This prompted me to write an article about the social rules that one should follow when changing a department style. The article was published in the March 2005 edition of TECHWR-L Magazine.

Here's an excerpt: "Creating a style guide may initially seem like a terminology affair ('option button' or 'radio button' — pick one), but the real challenge lies in persuading the department to adopt new style principles.

"Some writers will feel threatened by change, and respond in bizarre and unpredictable ways. Whisper campaigns and ambushes may lie in wait for you. Beware, innovative editor! Before you even think about the literary details of style, prepare to do battle with the true Goliaths and Grendyls: the department itself. By following these five rules below, you can avoid an unexpected apocalypse when you reveal the new guide." Read the article.

Rereading it, I made it more sensational than it probably should have been. As an interesting sidenote, TECHRW-L recently converted their site into a Drupal blog, and they featured my blog feed in their list of interesting and relevant sites.

"10 Types of Expats"

After living in Egypt for two years, I ran across hundreds of different expatriates, and I began to see patterns emerge. I couldn't resist writing about it. This article was published in the May 2004 edition of the Expat Exchange.

Here's an excerpt describing one type of expat: "The Drifter: She decided to take a year abroad, not because she has specific plans to study Arabic or network ties at US AID, but because it 'looked like a good option at the time.'

"She finds herself sometimes wandering aimlessly about the streets, not really committed to any particular plan or agenda. She is just as happy to meet a local as another foreigner. In the back of her mind she believes in fate and destiny, though she may just shrug her shoulders about this if confronted.

"She hopes that something greater than herself has pulled her here. Around every corner she is waiting for that force of destiny to manifest itself in a convincing way. The drifter is your friend, but she is everyone's friend as well, because she is secretly searching for meaning, and wondering if she can find it with you, or with the person next to you." Read the article.

"John Bascom: Reviving Cowboy Reality"

I'm digging back a few years with this article, but it was a memorable one. In college I worked as a writer/editor for the alumni house, and as such I published a number of alumni profiles for the web, newsletter, and magazine. This article and one other appeared in BYU's Spring 1999 magazine.

Here's an excerpt: "Beyond preserving cowboy culture in art, Bascom combines an interest in Church history with his love of the old West by organizing weekend cattle drives. The drives he creates give cowboy aficionados a taste of the realities of the West.

Web Projects

The following samples demonstrate various web projects I've completed. In some cases, I created the site and the content. In others, I supplied just the text and structured the flow of information.

STC-Suncoast Chapter site

As webmaster, I transformed the previously static site for the Suncoast STC chapter into collaborative WordPress chapter micro-CMS/blog to encourage more community participation from the members. "Six Key Facts About Protein Everyone Should Know"

Getting the facts and details of protein straight took me hours of research and mental organization. I didn't know it at the time, but my next employer, who happened to have a PhD in biology, hired me because she liked this article so much.

Here's an excerpt: "Amino acids not synthesized into protein become converted to calories or energy. However, converting protein to calories or energy requires your body to strip the amino acids of their nitrogen atoms, creating nitrogen waste. This nitrogen waste from the metabolized protein arrives at your kidneys as a toxic 'urea,' and must be filtered out." Read the article.

Ten Steps to Writing an Essay

This was one of my first websites, and I created it with Dreamweaver as an accompaniment for my composition classes while I taught at the American University in Cairo. It's an extensive how-to site for writing an essay, and includes interactive exercises and instruction on a variety of composition topics. I'm amazed at how popular the site still is. It gets an average of 1,500 hits a day.

When I wrote the home page, I search-engine-optimized the copy, so if you google "steps + essay" it's the first return on the list. This site makes me realize how impressions on the web left long ago can remain just as fresh as if we'd made them yesterday.

The university later changed the site's banner (which now hides some of the side navigation of the home page), and I no longer have access to the file directory, but it's still an excellent resource for learning to write essays.

Here's an excerpt: "When you conduct research, move from light to thorough resources to make sure you're moving in the right direction. Begin by doing searches on the Internet about your topic to familiarize yourself with the basic issues; then move to more thorough research on the Academic Databases; finally, probe the depths of the issue by burying yourself in the library.

"Make sure that despite beginning on the Internet, you don't simply end there. A research paper using only Internet sources is a weak paper, and puts you at a disadvantage for not using better information from more academic sources. " www.aucegypt.edu/academic/writers
