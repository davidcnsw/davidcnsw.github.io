I've been reading Robert Horn's book on visual language. The premise is that communication through visual imagery follows enough conventions and techniques that it constitutes a language in an of itself. Horn gets too much into morphology, syntax, other linguistic theory than I want to learn. But I like the angle Horn explores.

Conceptual diagrams do a great job at bringing text to life. If there's one goal I have as a technical writer, it's to integrate more visual language into my deliverables. I'm not just referring to screenshots, but to concept diagrams. As I'm explaining the big picture behind a system, or the process, or some other concept, a visual image helps clarify that topic in significant ways. The clarity achieved through visuals benefits both users and information creators.

Why don't we include more visual diagrams in our help material? For starters, creating a visual diagram is hard work. Technical writers barely have time as it is to finish the documentation, let alone include dozens of hand-drawn images. Another reason is that technical writers generally don't have an artist's skills to illustrate. After 3rd grade, we stopped believing in our ability to draw, paint, or create.

I recently created some quick reference guides that are heavily image based. Working on images is the counterpart to documentation that I've always neglected. Partly it's because I couldn't create the illustrations for lack of graphics app know-how. But another problem is conceptualizing what image can be used to convey n idea.
