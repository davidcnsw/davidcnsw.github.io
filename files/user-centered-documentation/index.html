10 patterns of user behavior with doc

  1. Users are frustrated, impatient
  2. Users read non-sequentially
  3. Users organize the same items differently
  4. Some users are beginners
  5. Some users are advanced
  6. Users resist going into help
  7. Users desire simple instructions
  8. Users want to begin right away
  9. Users gravitate toward visuals
  10. Users may not speak your "language"

— Can't do usability studies every time

— Instead, optimize for universal behaviors

— List isn't exhaustive or scientific

P1: Users read non-sequentially

You can just read the sections about the tasks you want to do.
— John Carroll, The Nurnberg Funnel

— Published in 1990

— Reaction against sequential learning models at the time

— Realized readers skip around out of sequence, impatient to act

Topic-based authoring is great. Where do things go wrong?

— Minimalism formed basis for topic-based authoring, structured authoring

— Seems like a great idea, but can go wrong

— 2 main reasons why

Wrong: Building blocks ≠ presentation

— DITA topic types not glued together

— Result is massive information fragmentation

— User plays information pinball in help

Wrong: Don't auto-burst print books

— Auto-bursting is arbitrary division at heading level

— Common in transition from print to online

— Write content with the primary pub format in mind

The Frankenbook

A Frankenbook is organized neither for linear reading, nor for random access. No matter where you land in it, you are in the middle of a maze with buttons to move up, down, or sideways, but no means of finding the end of any thread of narrative, great or small. Every page is page 297 and none of them answer your question .... .
— Mark Baker, Every Page Is Page One

— Mark Baker's book is reaction to info fragmentation in doc

— Frankenbook is online help stitched together but not really functional

— Classic example: arrive at topic but can't do anything

Strategy: Treat "Every Page Is Page One"

— Users arrive at any point -- usually from search

— How functional is doc in this paradigm?

— How do you write help for this?

Characteristics of "Every Page Is Page One" topics:

  • Self-contained
  • Specific and limited purpose
  • Conform to type
  • Establish context
  • Assume the reader is qualified
  • Stay on one level
  • Link richly

— Dives into detail in book

— Every topic can be set in some larger context

— Draw the line somewhere. Provide necessary context.

Strategy: Create self-contained topics

— WordPress installation page really long

— Encyclopedic wikis lend themselves to EPPO style

— Topics more thorough, standalone

Strategy: Provide context before

— Simple graphic but incredibly helpful

— No need to put all this content on the same page

— Essential for sequential processes

Strategy: Provide context after

— What comes next? Handhold user

— Helps provide context

— Only necessary for sequence, but also helpful in other topics

P2: Users feel frustrated, impatient

How Users Read on the Web

Summary: They don't. People rarely read Web pages word by word; instead, they scan the page, picking out individual words and sentences.
— Jakob Nielsen, Nielsen Norman Group

— Consume content at a glance

— Users trying to get to right page

— Avg. time on page for most is under 2 sec

Strategy: Use article summaries

— Supports wayfinding behavior -- is this the page I want?

— Very helpful on Nielsen's site

— Similar to shordesc element in DITA. Summary can appear in many places.

Strategy: Use lots of subheadings

UX Matters

— One of most useful techniques in writing

— Microcosm of page summary applied at paragraph level

— Subheads should be clear -- this helps focus writing too

Strategy: Structure information in lists

— Even non-list paragraphs can be restructured as lists

— Bold intro. sentences facilitate scanning

— Dramatically improves readability

Unreadable content — no list formatting

Nebraska is filled with internationally recognized attractions that draw large crowds of people every year, without fail. In 1996, some of the most popular places were Fort Robinson State Park (355,000 visitors), Scotts Bluff National Monument (132,166), Arbor Lodge State Historical Park & Museum (100,000), Carhenge (86,598), Stuhr Museum of the Prairie Pioneer (60,002), and Buffalo Bill Ranch State Historical Park (28,446).

— No list

— Not concise

— People gloss over long walls of text

Rewritten for readability — 124% usability improvement

In 1996, six of the most-visited places in Nebraska were:

  • Fort Robinson State Park
  • Scotts Bluff National Monument
  • Arbor Lodge State Historical Park & Museum
  • Carhenge
  • Stuhr Museum of the Prairie Pioneer
  • Buffalo Bill Ranch State Historical Park

Jakob Nielsen, How Users read on the web

— List structure improves readability

— Also more concise

— Facilitates scanning

Strategy: Make sentences short and easy

We're thinking "great literature" (or at least "product brochure"), while the user’s reality is much closer to "billboard going by at 60 miles an hour." — Steve Krug, Don't Make Me Think

— Sentences need to be short b/c of reading speed

— Can you consume all content on page at a glance?

— Billboard text is really short

P3: Users organize same items differently

— Classic usability technique to group content

— People still disagree about how to arrange

— People may not even understand the product

Where would you find this in a store?

— Dessert aisle, coconuts, ethic, canned goods

— We group and classify based on our own needs, assumptions

— Can never find anything in stores

Physical spaces vs. digital spaces

— David Weinberger's Everything Is Miscellaneous

— Classification systems break down, groupings don't work

— Better to use metadata, let users pull based on facets they want

Faceted navigation

Faceted navigation is arguably the most significant search innovation of the past decade.
— Peter Morville, Search Patterns

— How faceted navigation works

— Why is it such a big deal?

— Maybe a little over-hyped

Strategy: Leverage facets if they make sense

— Works best if you have physical products with attributes

— For info, facets might be model, brand, version

— More applicable to a doc portal than standalone help

Tags are practical facets

With tags, your files and photos can be in two, three, or more “places” at once.
— Gene Smith, People-Powered Metadata for the Social Web

— Almost every web system has tags

— Tags allow for other groupings apart from TOC

— Easy to implement, but only add meaningful tags

Strategy: Use tags to provide other groupings

— Tags can surface related pages

— You wouldn't have an Active Directory TOC in column

— I like the idea of a metadata column on pages

Another example of tags

— Tags work well for larger information systems

— Suppose I want to read another book like Catcher in Rye

— Don't go overboard with tagging

Hierarchical Table of Contents fail

from Mark Baker's post here: http://everypageispageone.com/2013/03/19/web-organization-is-not-like-book-organization.

— TOCs work only for small systems

— Topics have multiple relationships with each other

— TOC can't capture these relationships, fails

Strategy: Provide "Bottom-up" navigation

Example from "Every Page Is Page One

— Just means linking within a topic

— We arrive at the topic, from bottom

— We don't think hierarchically, drilling through TOC levels

P4: Some users are beginners

— Common type of user

— Beginners need diff. types of content

Strategy: Create "getting started" guides

— Outlines first steps in application

— This should be a required section in any guide

Also "Getting Started" Guides

— Prod. mgr cried when gave him this

— Tech writer finally understands that users don't want long manuals

— Popular during trainings, introductions

Strategy: Provide sequential tutorials

— For beginner wanting to learn, sequence is okay

— Beginners can't understand more complex topics initially

— Learning Java, you'd be lost if going into reference docs

Strategy: Give the big picture

— We get so focused on details, lose sight of big picture

— Narrative workflow topics address this scenario

— Step back. How and why does system work overall?

P5: Some users are advanced

— People who have been using application

— Also tech savvy people who think they don't need instructions

— Help needs to go beyond obvious to support these types

Strategy: Create "quick reference" guides

— Differs from getting started -- quick summary of functions

— Like cliff notes or cheat sheet

— Especially helpful for lists of functions, classes, methods

Strategy: Emphasize troubleshooting information

— Advanced users consult help in troubleshooting

— Troubleshooting should be standard in any help

— When documenting systems, go outside the happy path

StackOverflow focuses on troubleshooting

— Immense popularity b/c focuses on problems

— If can't find answer, add it. Next time user searches, answer appears

— Each topic is standalone. Good example of EPPO

Strategy: Use homepage as routing page

— Homepage should be train station routing people

— Most popular page in help shouldn't focus only on beginning user

— Homepage is a pathfinding page

P6: Users resist going into help

The conclusion of most studies about how people use help is that people don't use help.
-- Mike Hughes, I'd Rather Be Writing podcast

Audio excerpt

— One of most memorable podcasts

— Usability studies artificial

— Way to get people to use help is to suggest it in difficult tasks

Fitt's Law

The further a user must travel with their cursor, the less accuracy the user will have in reaching the target object.

— Travis Lowdermilk, User-centered Design

— If answer is far from problem area, less likely to find

— Proximity of info helps users find and use it

— Help doesn't need to be separate

Strategy: Bring help into the UI

— Simple popovers or tooltips work well

— Provide UX designers with JSON file to act as content API

— This is easiest way to do in-app help

Strategy: Make the UI more helpful

— Look at all the text

— UX doesn't want to overload b/c text only helpful the first time

— Tech writers should play larger role in UI than they usually do

Half of the UI is text

— Useless without text

— The UI is domain where wordsmiths should reign

WordPress blanked out

Youtube blanked out

Strategy: Show tasks in the UI

— Walkhub

— Requires a lot more programming, but kind of nifty

— Allow users to learn while remaining in task domain

P7: Users desire simple instructions

The same technology that simplifies life by providing more functions in each device also complicates life by making the device harder to learn, harder to use. This is the paradox of technology.

— Don Norman, The Design of Everyday Things

— Impact on help -- users want simple, but complexity needs doc

— If help is too brief, it's equally frustrating

— Paradox of information -- more is less, less is more

Documentation for a complex product

— Users often won't even read if too big

— Martin Fowler's article: The Almighty Thud --> dread

Special outputs per role?

— Classic approach, but more suited for print paradigms

— Online outputs get confused when users arrive via search

— Relevant if clearly defined roles, but not if roles fuzzy

Strategy: Provide filtering mechanisms

Include it all. Filter it afterward.
— Dave Weinberger, Too Big to Know

— Reduce complexity by providing filtering

— A good search with facets that can narrow

— When TOC is scrapped, why not include more?

Strategy: Apply progressive information disclosure

Progressive disclosure is the best tool so far: show people the basics first, and once they understand that, allow them to get to the expert features. But don't show everything all at once or you will only confuse people and they will waste endless time messing with features that they don't need yet.
— Jakob Nielsen, Interaction Design

— Major principle of usability

— Showing all info at once produces info paralysis

— Cognitive overload sets in as users must process items

Example of progressive information disclosure

— Makes help simpler, easier to navigate

— Very basic architectural tools

An example of progressive disclosure in the UI

— Spoonfeed in UI; if more needed, click links

— Can apply PD at various levels -- in app, in help, in topic

Strategy: Create different sidebars for different sections

— Not so common in HATs

— Mini-TOC makes it more navigable

— Get away from model where every page has same TOC

P8: Users want to begin right away

The premise behind minimalism is that people learning to use computer software are impatient, mentally active, and curious. They want to begin right away getting their work done.

— David Farkas and Thomas Williams summarizing John Carroll

— People usually start in app before going to help

— Leaving app feels unproductive

— Help people feel productive immediately

Strategy: Focus on real-world tasks

— The First Minimal Manual

— Content must address real user's tasks

Strategy: Provide hello-world tutorials

— Carryover from API doc world with high relevance

— Get some output with system in the most minimal way

— Usually requires a workflow involve a series of independent topics

Strategy: Keep the focus in the task domain

— The more you can put help in reader's focus, the better

— Problem here is that user is given help prior to asking

— Turn on or off help in application

P9: Users gravitate toward visuals

— Primary realization from usability sessions

— Visuals more suited to beginners

— One of most neglected aspects of doc

Strategy: Use visuals to reduce cognitive load

— Visuals manage cognitive load

— Working memory can only hold so much

— Dual encoding (adding audio) boosts learning

Chartjunk doesn't enhance learning

— Chartjunk is decorative graphics

— Graphics need to do work of learning

— Chartjunk can actually detract/hurt learning

Show workflows and processes

— Workflows very helpful, easy to create

— Embed these into topics as image maps

Strategy: Reduce distraction, focus on the point

— Photos have distracting detail

— Simpler the better

Strategy: Worry about ideas, not high art

— Dan Roam + others all say this

— Hard part is coming up with illustration idea

— Everything else just fancy execution of idea

P10: Users may not speak your "language"

Science has become more complex, more specialized—every sub-discipline has its own vocabulary.
— Stephen Hall, How Scientists Are Learning to Write

— Every product has its own terminology

— Left-align, full justification, resizing -- we learn terms

— Recognize jargon

Peter Morville: Each search informs the next

— Users learn terms via searches

— Find way within help to teach users the terms

— Scent of information

Strategy: Define unfamiliar terms in a glossary

— Should be standard part of any help

— Glossaries help expose terms and orient reader

Strategy: Put keywords, synonyms in page metadata

Add keywords in topics


---
title: Access ACME
permalink: /access/
tags: [getting-started]
keywords: logging in, authentication, login
---

Push keywords into page metadata

— This is Jekyll frontmatter

— Simple searches read page metadata tags

— Figure it out in your system

Strategy: Write for users who *really* don't speak your language

— Global audience with ESL

— Short sentences, active voice

— Other tips in writing for translation

How to test content

Give users a problem to solve with your docs

— Alex Gaynor from Write the Docs Portland

— Just as code needs QA, doc needs QA

— QA for accuracy and usability

Observe users do tasks during training sessions

— Users get learning, you get usability feedback

— Give tasks to do at start, let them try to figure out

— Watch as they use the app

Test users directly on reader comprehension

— Don't trust yes/no, like/dislike answers

— Probe how well your content teaches

— More quantatative results
